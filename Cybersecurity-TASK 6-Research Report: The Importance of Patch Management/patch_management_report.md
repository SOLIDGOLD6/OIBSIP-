# The Importance of Patch Management

## Introduction

Patch management is the organizational process of identifying, prioritizing, acquiring, testing, deploying, and verifying software updates ("patches") that fix security vulnerabilities, bugs, or missing functionality across an enterprise's applications, operating systems, and firmware. <cite index="92-1">NIST defines enterprise patch management as the process of identifying, prioritizing, acquiring, installing, and verifying the installation of patches, updates, and upgrades throughout an organization, framing it not as a discretionary IT task but as a critical component of preventive maintenance for computing technology — a basic cost of doing business.</cite> Patch management sits at the center of the broader vulnerability management lifecycle: a vulnerability is discovered (by a researcher, vendor, or attacker), it is disclosed and cataloged, a vendor releases a fix, and organizations must then apply that fix before attackers exploit the gap. The window between "a patch exists" and "the patch is applied everywhere it's needed" is precisely the attack surface this report is about — and, as the case studies below show, that window is where some of the most damaging breaches in history have occurred.

## Why Patches Matter: Discovery, Disclosure, and Exploitation

Vulnerabilities are typically discovered through one of several paths: internal code review by the software vendor, external security researchers conducting responsible disclosure, bug bounty programs, or — worst of all — discovery by attackers who exploit the flaw before anyone else knows about it (a "zero-day"). Once a vulnerability is confirmed, it is generally assigned a Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) identifier through MITRE's CVE program, giving the flaw a unique, universally recognized reference number (e.g., CVE-2017-0144) so that vendors, researchers, and defenders can track it consistently across advisories, scanners, and patches. Each CVE is typically also scored using the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), a standardized 0–10 scale maintained by FIRST.org that rates severity based on factors like attack vector, complexity, privileges required, and impact on confidentiality, integrity, and availability — giving organizations a consistent basis for prioritizing which vulnerabilities to patch first. The problem is that publishing a CVE and a patch does not close the vulnerability — it only starts a race, because the same disclosure that tells defenders how to fix a flaw also tells attackers how to exploit it in every system that hasn't yet applied the fix.

Two real-world breaches illustrate this race being lost.

**WannaCry / EternalBlue (2017).** <cite index="82-1">EternalBlue was an exploit developed by the NSA that targeted a vulnerability in Microsoft's implementation of the Server Message Block (SMB) protocol, catalogued as CVE-2017-0144.</cite> <cite index="83-1">Microsoft had already issued a fix for the underlying flaw in security bulletin MS17-010 on March 14, 2017, and systems that applied it were no longer vulnerable to the exploit.</cite> <cite index="82-1">However, after the Shadow Brokers group publicly leaked the EternalBlue exploit code on April 14, 2017, the WannaCry ransomware worm used it on May 12, 2017 to attack Windows computers that had not received the update.</cite> <cite index="89-1">Because unpatched computers exposing the SMB protocol could be infected simply by being powered on and reachable — no user action required — the worm spread automatically across networks.</cite> <cite index="85-1">Within days it infected more than 200,000 machines across over 150 countries, disrupting production lines at Renault and Nissan, halting a Honda plant, and causing the UK's National Health Service to confirm 6,912 cancelled appointments and operations, with total NHS-related damage estimated at £19 million.</cite> The patch had existed for two full months before the attack.

**Equifax (2017).** <cite index="105-1">On March 7, 2017, the Apache Software Foundation publicly disclosed CVE-2017-5638, a critical remote-code-execution vulnerability in the Apache Struts web framework, and released a patch the same day.</cite> <cite index="109-1">The U.S. Department of Homeland Security (US-CERT) notified Equifax of the vulnerability the very next day, and an internal email instructing administrators to apply the patch went out on March 9 — yet a subsequent internal vulnerability scan on March 15 failed to detect the still-unpatched systems.</cite> <cite index="106-1">Three days after disclosure, on March 10, attackers exploited the exact same vulnerability on Equifax's online dispute portal, then moved laterally through the network for months, ultimately exfiltrating personal data on 147.9 million people between May and July 2017.</cite> <cite index="110-1">Equifax eventually reached a global settlement of up to $700 million with the FTC, the CFPB, and all 50 state attorneys general.</cite> As with WannaCry, the failure was not a lack of a patch — <cite index="102-1">a patch existed and scanning tools could detect the vulnerability; Equifax simply failed to apply the fix in time.</cite>

## Consequences of Not Patching

The two cases above are not outliers; they are representative of an industry-wide pattern with severe and quantifiable consequences:

- **Data breaches and ransomware**: Unpatched, internet-facing vulnerabilities remain one of the most common initial access vectors used by both opportunistic and targeted attackers, precisely because they require no social engineering and can often be scanned for and exploited automatically at scale, as WannaCry demonstrated.
- **Financial penalties**: <cite index="110-1">Equifax's settlement broke down into up to $425 million for a consumer restitution fund, $175 million to the states, and a $100 million civil penalty to the CFPB</cite> — on top of legal, remediation, and reputational costs that followed for years.
- **Operational disruption**: <cite index="85-1">WannaCry's self-propagation halted physical manufacturing lines and forced the cancellation of thousands of medical appointments and operations</cite>, showing that unpatched IT vulnerabilities can cascade into real-world, safety-relevant consequences well beyond data loss.
- **Compliance violations**: <cite index="97-1">Patch management is required by numerous security compliance frameworks; for example, NIST SP 800-53's SI-2 (Flaw Remediation) control mandates installing and testing security-relevant patches, and the PCI DSS sets maximum timeframes for installing the most critical patches</cite>, meaning failure to patch can itself constitute a compliance violation independent of whether a breach occurs. <cite index="95-1">CISA, GSA, and NIST commonly recommend remediation timeframes of 30 days for high-severity vulnerabilities, 90 days for medium, and 120 days for low-severity findings.</cite>
- **Reputational and executive-level damage**: <cite index="108-1">Beyond the financial settlement, Equifax faced congressional hearings, multiple lawsuits, and a long-lasting blow to its reputation</cite>, and both incidents remain widely cited case studies of patch management failure years later.

## The Patch Management Lifecycle

NIST SP 800-40 Revision 4 frames patch management as a continuous risk-management cycle rather than a one-time task. The core phases are:

1. **Discovery (Identification)** — <cite index="101-1">knowing when new software vulnerabilities affect the organization's assets, including applications, operating systems, and firmware</cite>. This depends on maintaining a complete, current inventory of what software and versions are actually deployed, and on monitoring vulnerability feeds (CVE/NVD, vendor advisories, CISA alerts) so new disclosures are mapped against that inventory immediately.
2. **Assessment (Planning the risk response)** — <cite index="101-1">assessing the risk a given vulnerability poses to the organization, choosing an appropriate risk response, and deciding how to implement it</cite>, typically using CVSS scores combined with organization-specific context (is the asset internet-facing? does it hold sensitive data? is there a known exploit in the wild?) to prioritize what gets patched first, deferred, or handled with a compensating control.
3. **Testing** — validating that a patch does not break critical functionality before wide deployment, usually in a staging environment that mirrors production, since a patch that causes an outage can be as disruptive as the vulnerability it fixes.
4. **Deployment (Implementing the risk response)** — <cite index="93-1">distributing and installing the patch, deploying additional security controls, or changing asset configuration and state as appropriate</cite>, on a schedule coordinated with change management and business units to minimize disruption while closing the exposure window as quickly as the assessed risk warrants.
5. **Verification and Monitoring** — <cite index="93-1">confirming a patch has been installed successfully and taken effect, typically through automated means at scale, and then continuously monitoring to confirm the patch remains installed</cite> — since a patch can later be silently undone by a user rollback, an attacker, or a device being restored from an outdated backup or factory-default state.

## Best Practices: A Prioritized 7-Step Patch Management Checklist

1. **Maintain a complete, continuously updated asset inventory** — you cannot patch what you don't know you have; this is the prerequisite for every other step, and its absence was a documented contributor to Equifax's failed vulnerability scan.
2. **Subscribe to and triage vulnerability intelligence daily** — monitor CVE/NVD feeds, vendor advisories, and CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog so newly disclosed, actively exploited vulnerabilities are flagged against your inventory immediately rather than discovered during a routine monthly scan.
3. **Risk-rank every vulnerability using CVSS plus business context** — combine the CVSS base score with factors like internet exposure, data sensitivity, and known exploitation to set a remediation deadline (e.g., 30/90/120 days for high/medium/low severity), rather than patching in the order vulnerabilities happen to be reported.
4. **Establish emergency ("out-of-cycle") patching procedures for critical, actively exploited flaws** — normal change-management timelines are too slow for a CVE already being weaponized in the wild (as EternalBlue was); define a fast-track approval path in advance so it doesn't have to be improvised under pressure.
5. **Test patches in a representative staging environment before production rollout** — balancing the risk of delay against the risk of an untested patch breaking a critical system, with a defined rollback plan if issues appear.
6. **Automate deployment and verification wherever possible** — manual patching does not scale and is the most common reason patches silently fail to apply across an entire fleet; automated patch management tooling should confirm installation, not just initiate it.
7. **Continuously verify and re-audit** — schedule recurring vulnerability scans and penetration tests to confirm patches remain applied over time, catching regressions, rollbacks, and previously unknown assets that slipped past the original inventory.

## Challenges and How to Overcome Them

- **Legacy and unsupported systems** — Older systems may no longer receive vendor patches at all, or a patch may be incompatible with dependent legacy software. *Mitigation:* isolate legacy systems through network segmentation, apply compensating controls (e.g., disabling unnecessary services like SMBv1), and build a funded, time-bound modernization plan rather than allowing indefinite exceptions.
- **Downtime concerns** — Business units often resist patching windows that could interrupt production systems, especially in always-on environments. *Mitigation:* use redundant/clustered architectures so patches can be rolled out to one node at a time, negotiate pre-agreed maintenance windows in advance, and reserve emergency out-of-cycle patching for only the highest-severity, actively exploited cases to preserve trust in the process.
- **Testing requirements and change friction** — Thorough testing takes time, but delaying too long extends the exposure window, as seen at Equifax. *Mitigation:* maintain a staging environment that mirrors production closely enough to catch most regressions quickly, and tier the testing rigor to the severity of the vulnerability — critical, actively exploited flaws warrant an expedited test-and-deploy path, while low-risk patches can follow the normal cycle.
- **Organizational and communication gaps** — Even when a patch and a notification exist, the responsible team may never receive or act on it, as happened when Equifax's internal alert process broke down. *Mitigation:* assign clear, named ownership for patch management per asset class, route vulnerability alerts to a maintained and verified distribution list, and track remediation against defined SLAs with management visibility so gaps are caught before they become breaches.
- **Incomplete or inaccurate asset inventory** — Unknown or unmanaged assets cannot be patched or even scanned effectively. *Mitigation:* combine automated discovery/CAASM (Cyber Asset Attack Surface Management) tooling with periodic manual reconciliation, and require any new asset onboarded to the network to be registered in inventory as a hard prerequisite for connectivity.
